history Defense budgets, burden-sharing & rearmament (modelled)

These are synthetic defense-spending series shaped to resemble major geopolitical eras: Cold War burden-sharing, the 1990s peace dividend, post-2001 expeditionary buildup, and the renewed rearmament cycle after 2014 and especially after 2022.

Common reasons spending rises

  • Cold War bloc competition — sustained high defense burdens across NATO, the USSR, and key Asian allies.
  • Post-2001 expeditionary campaigns — US-led operations lifted spending and procurement across several allies.
  • 2014 Crimea and 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine — European rearmament, munitions restocking, and higher burden-sharing targets.

Common reasons spending falls

  • 1990s peace dividend — many advanced economies cut force structure and procurement after the Soviet collapse.
  • Fiscal crises and austerity — debt stress often delayed replacement cycles and capital modernization.
  • Demographic and social spending pressures — aging populations can crowd out defense growth even when threats rise.

history Phone & internet contract costs (modelled)

Phone costs transition from flat landline fees toward rising mobile/data plans through the 1990s–2010s. Broadband barely existed before 1993; dial-up gave way to DSL, cable, then fibre. Deregulation dynamics differ sharply between markets.

Competition driving prices down

  • France 2012 — Iliad (Free Mobile) — disruptive market entry slashed mobile prices; similar dynamics later in UK and Nordics.
  • South Korea & Japan broadband investment (2000s) — government-backed fibre build-outs drove down per-Mb costs sharply.

Prices remaining high

  • North American mobile markets — limited operator competition kept USD plan prices well above comparable European tariffs.
  • Data plan inflation (2010s+) — shift from voice-only to unlimited-data contracts drove average revenue per user upward.

history TV & total connectivity bundle costs (modelled)

Pay TV expanded from a US-centric model in the 1970s to global cable through the 1990s. Streaming disruption created new subscription layers; remaining cable subscribers often paid more as audience bases shrank.

Elective surgery wait times are a proxy for public system capacity; physician density (WB indicator SH.MED.PHYS.ZS) tracks health workforce supply. High-income single-payer systems often face queues the US fee-for-service model avoids—at a price.

System capacity gains

  • Post-war hospital building (1950s–1980s) — most OECD countries expanded beds and trained more physicians.
  • Korea & UAE investment (1990s–2010s) — rapid growth in physician numbers from a low base.

System stress

  • COVID-19 surgical backlogs (2020–2023) — elective procedures deferred globally; waits surged, especially in NHS-style systems.
  • Austerity (2010–2015) — health workforce hiring freezes in several European countries.

history Hospital Capacity & Healthcare Spending

Bed counts reflect historic hospital-building eras (Japan, Russia still carry Soviet-era density) and now-dominant moves toward outpatient care. Spending per capita can rise without proportional outcome gains if prices, not utilization, drive costs.

Capacity expansion

  • Korea & China (1990s–2010s) — dramatic bed growth as economies urbanised and middle classes demanded hospital care.
  • Health spending growth — OECD countries consistently increased real per-capita expenditure.

Rationalisation & pressure

  • OECD bed reduction (1980s+ onwards) — shift to day surgery and community care cut inpatient stays.
  • US spending outlier — highest per-capita spending with average life expectancy among peers.

history Christian & Muslim Population Shares (modelled)

These are illustrative long-run trajectories based on Pew Research demographic projections and national census data. Religious identity changes slowly via births, deaths, immigration, and voluntary switching (secularisation). Year-to-year precision is low.

Broad trends

  • Christian share declining in Western Europe & North America — secularisation and immigration diversification from the 1970s onward.
  • Muslim share growing in Western Europe — immigration from MENA and South Asia; higher birth rates in the first generation.

Limitations to note

  • Census methodology varies — self-identification vs. state religion registration produces different totals.
  • UAE & Malaysia — high Muslim share reflects indigenous majority (Malaysia) and expatriate composition (UAE).

history Secular & Eastern Traditions (modelled)

Secularisation is among the most consistent long-run social trends in high-income countries; the "nones" (no religion) are a fast-growing category in surveys. Hindu and Buddhist shares are relatively stable regionally but shift with diaspora migration patterns.

Secularisation wave

  • Nordic countries — very high secular share by 2000s; cultural Christianity without formal belief.
  • South Korea — uniquely high and rising "no religion" share alongside both Christianity and Buddhism.

Regional contrasts

  • India — Hindu majority extremely stable at ~80%; communal politics shape official identity data.
  • Japan — high "secular" in surveys but Buddhist & Shintō practices remain embedded in life-cycle rituals.
